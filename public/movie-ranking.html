Favorite Movies

My top 5 favorites

Dr. Strangelove or:
How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the Bomb

Screenplay: Terry Southern, Stanley Kubrick and Peter George
Director: Stanley Kubrick

An incredible tour de force of cinematic writing, directing and acting, this film succeeded in doing the unthinkable—treating the prospect of world thermonuclear war as comedy. Of course, it is a very dark, black comedic treatment, but hysterically funny, nonetheless. Additionally, it is a profound anti-war statement, not the first released by Director Stanley Kubrick.

The film was completed at the height of the Cold War in 1963 and originally intended for release that year. But the real-world tragedy on November 22, 1963—the assasination of President Kennedy—made the release far too controversial given the turmoil immediately following Kennedy's death, and the continued fears of nuclear weapons proliferation. The film was released instead on January 29, 1964.

Paths of Glory

Screenplay: Stanley Kubrick, Calder Willingham and Jim Thompson
Director: Stanley Kubrick

Released in 1957, and also directed by Stanley Kubrick, this film, starring Kirk Douglas, dared to show the horrors of trench warfare during World War I (The Great War or The War to End All Wars, as it was known at the time). It is a great anti-war statement which preceded that shown in Dr. Strangelove.

The story centers around "Colonel Dax, the commanding officer of French soldiers who refuse to continue a suicidal attack, after which Dax attempts to defend them against charges of cowardice in a court-martial" (Wikipedia).

The Wizard of Oz

Screenplay: Noel Langley, Florence Ryerson and Edgar Allan Woolf
Director: Victor Fleming

Released in 1939, the year Hitler invaded Poland, this timeless classic, no doubt, provided many with a respite from the increasingly grim news coming out of Europe. The film never gets old on repeated viewing, and tells the story of Dorothy, whose dog Toto is accused by Almira Gulch of having bitten her. The wealthy Gulch obtains an order from the local sherriff allowing her to destory the dog.

Dorothy runs away with Toto to protect him from Mrs. Gulch and gets caught in a tornado. When she returns home, she is struck in the head by a window that flies off the house and is she is knocked unconscious. In her vision, Mrs. Gulch turns into the Wicked Witch of the West, who pursues her through the magical land of Oz trying to avenge the death of her sister, an act that she blames on Dorothy.

Among the many distinctions of this story of magical realism brought to film is that this is the first film in history to use technicolor.

Amadeus

Screenplay: Peter Shaffer
Director: Miloš Forman

Released in 1984, Amadeus is a fictionalized accounting of the trials and tribulations of Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart and the devious plot of Antonio Salieri, contemporary composer in Mozart's time, to kill him.

The story, told throgh the eyes of Italian composer Salieri, includes many selections of both Mozart's and Salieri's composition. The part of Mozart was masterfully portrayed by Tom Hulce, and Salieri, played by F. Murray Abraham, earned him the Academy Award for Best Actor.

Topsy Turvy

Screenplay and Direction: Mike Leigh

Topsy Turvy, released in 1999, directed and written by Mike Leigh, chronicles the relationship between composer Arthur Sullivan and playwright/librettist W.S. (William Schwenk) Gilbert. Filled with the poignant struggles of historic figures of some performers of the D'Oyle Carte Opera company, the film centers around the tumultuous process of creating one of their most beloved satirical operettas, The Mikado.

Set in Japan, the Mikado is perhaps more acurately characterized as a biting satirical look at bourgeois English society of the Victorian era.
